Process Collecting User Data Game Prototyping

  1. Step 1: Ask questions

    The most important factor in crafting a successful UX is understanding your audience, and that means asking questions. The more narrowly you can define your audience, the more targeted your messaging can be. In short:

    Beautiful haikus
    won't impress the smartest dog,
    but a biscuit might.

    Email me for additional case studies

    Currently revamping this portfolio for 2017.

  2. Step 2: Draft a solution

    My favorite "low-fi" drafting tool is an iOS app called Paper . I feel like having access to color allows me to be a little more expressive without the effort of tracking down a variety of pens, and working in a digital format enables me to share, import, and iterate on my designs without wasting any time.

    Drafts usually start with a basic objective in mind - "Solve for X", if you will. Solving for any individual challenge is pretty easy; the real work is in solving for many, occasionally conflicting, Xs all at once.

  3. Step 3: Wireframe & Prototype

    As I drafted a mock-up with Axure, I realized that my solution did solve some issues, but it still didn't give me the ability to talk about my process. Undeterred, I did a little research ("asked questions") and designed something new.

    It's possible to catch design issues at any stage in the process, so for small teams, wireframing isn't always necessary. Even so, it's usually worthwhile because the sooner you notice something is wrong, the less it costs to fix it.

    Click here to see my (rough) prototype

  4. Step 4: Build & Test

    Not only can I design it, but I can build it too! As an experienced Front-End Developer with a solid understanding of browsers and of my website, I knew which designs I could implement quickly, and what changes I'd need to make to keep my portfolio responsive.

    This is where the designers typically step out of the picture, but when I'm working, I can iterate upon my design at any point. Dev. Tools are invaluable here, because you can quickly swap in an out images and text to test a variety of scenarios and content without risking any of your existing code.

  5. Step 5: Publish, Observe, & Iterate

    Observing first-time user behavior can often be tricky, because by now just about everybody on your team and their significant others have already seen your work. In this case I'll run my site past a few colleagues, and I'll wait to receive additional feedback from recruiters.

    User Experience Design is all about process: craft the best solution you can within time and budget constraints, observe people using your interface, research similar solutions, ask questions, and then find a way to make your solution better.

About Brian

Brian Donahue is a Seattle-based user experience designer and front-end developer; you may have seen some of his work at the 2014 Seattle Pride Festival or in your T‑Mobile bill. Brian believes that design is creative problem-solving, and he wants to use it to make the world a better place.

A life-long artist, passionate about his craft, Brian spent nearly 5 years as a barista perfecting his espresso skills, and currently volunteers as a brewing instructor at Street Bean Coffee. Brian has also volunteered his graphic design skills for several non-profits, and a previous employer said that "giving back is a huge part of [Brian's] ethos."
